Where do computers come from?

Art.

This sounds like a goofy answer to an obvious or trivial question but it is not. Before every new machine of any kind is put into production, be it a single prototype or mass production someone must decide to allocate the resources to build that machine instead of some other competing machine idea.

The artistic process by which a new machine is created usually progresses something like this:

  1. People discuss the concept with each other by writing on a whiteboard, sketching out diagrams to describe the idea
  2. After that art makes sense, it is put into a slide-based presentation for a "pitch" to the people with the money to allocate, be it program managers for academic research, investors for a startup, or senior management in a large company
  3. Engineers do the artwork required to make a pattern in silicon, niobium, aluminum or whatever which will embody the actual machine. While this will be a mundane task for updates to an existing machine, for a new machine it is much closer to art than pure engineering, and it is every bit as much as a graphics project as any other artwork would be.
  4. When the technology is first built, if it's a commercial product, its further replication depends on sales, which depends on the artwork created by the marketing team. This is treated as non-fundamental in computer science. However, an objective examination of technology history shows that this is indeed fundamental. The technology which is not replicated dies, and replication is based on large numbers of people making a buying decision, which is almost always strongly influenced by graphic arts.
  5. In order for the technology to compete in the marketplace of ideas and replicate in the larger world outside the creating lab/company, the creators must communicate their ideas clearly, which will generally involve significant streamlining of the earlier graphics to make art that both communicates the ideas to colleagues who can replicate it and casts a wider net in technology, making it clear to non-specialists whose fields intersect with that of the machine in question.
The premise of quantum noise dot org is that quantum computers are like every other technology: they are created and reproduce by art made on a classical computer. Furthermore, if we are using a computer simply to communicate art to another human, the figure of merit is how many people we can reach, not the "computing" power. For this purpose it makes to replace the word "computer" with something like "art engine" to make it clear what the purpose of the machine is.

As an art engine, the modern web, based on html5, css3, and JavaScript is the most powerful by several orders of magnitude. With just a few lines of fairly simple code, it is possible to make a document now that uses animation, graphics, sound, $\LaTeX$ typesetting of math, links to every other document of the same time, and numerous other features, and all those can be readable instantly by several billion people!

The codification of all information in this format has made more and more of society move at the speed of Twitter(for bettter or worse), while in applied science we still use an art stack that moves at the ponderous pace of the pre-web era. I define the "art stack" as the set of programs and systems we use to spread our art around in creation of technology. Right now a typical quantum computing art stack might consist of the following:

  • Dry erase board, scrap paper, lab notebook, napkins
  • Powerpoint, projector
  • Adobe Illustrator and photoshop
  • matlab, python, igor, mathematica, origin, or other similar graphing programs
  • The various software that interprets $\LaTeX$
  • CAD layout software for fabrication such as XIC, Cadence or DesignCAD
All of these tools are built for a maximally general audience, and none of them are developed for use in a totally web-based environment. I believe that by building a language of graphical art tools that are specific to the quantum information community .
